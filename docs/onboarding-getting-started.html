📖 Getting Started Guide

For first-time Daisy troop leaders. Plain language, practical advice.

🌼 What Is a Daisy Troop?

Daisies are the youngest Girl Scouts — kindergartners and first graders (ages 5–7). The program is named after Juliette Gordon Low's nickname, "Daisy." She founded Girl Scouts in 1912 because she believed every girl deserved the chance to develop courage, confidence, and character.

Your troop of 8 girls is a perfect size. Small enough to know every girl, big enough for team activities. You'll meet regularly, earn petals and badges, and — most importantly — have fun together.

💡 Key insight: At this age, the girls don't care about badges. They care about belonging, having fun, and doing cool stuff. The badges are your framework, not theirs. Use them to structure activities, but let the girls experience them as play and adventure.

🎯 What "Girl-Led" Means for 6-Year-Olds

GSUSA's core philosophy is "girl-led." For teens, this means girls plan their own service projects. For 6-year-olds, it looks very different — and that's okay.

Girl-Led at the Daisy Level Means:

  • Choices, not planning. "Should we do the craft first or the game first?" Not "Plan our entire meeting."
  • Voting with their feet. If an activity isn't working, pivot. Their attention span is your feedback.
  • Simple decision-making. "Which two petals should we work on next?" Give them 3 options, they pick 2.
  • Sharing what they like. "What was your favorite part?" helps you plan future meetings.
  • Taking small roles. "Who wants to be our flag holder today?" "Who wants to lead the Promise?"

What Girl-Led Does NOT Mean at This Age:

  • Girls planning meetings or setting agendas
  • Girls choosing all activities (they'll pick candy every time)
  • Letting chaos reign because "the girls are leading"
  • Feeling guilty that you're structuring things
💡 The formula: You set the structure. You offer choices within that structure. The girls make those choices. That's girl-led for Daisies.

🏕️ Troop Leadership Basics

Your Role

You're a facilitator, not a teacher. You don't need to know everything — you need to be organized, enthusiastic, and willing to look things up. The Volunteer Toolkit (VTK) on the GSUSA website has meeting plans for every badge and petal.

Meeting Structure That Works

Every Daisy meeting follows roughly the same rhythm. Girls this age thrive on routine:

  1. Opening (5–10 min): Gather, flag ceremony or Girl Scout Promise, attendance
  2. Activity 1 (15–20 min): Main badge/petal activity (discussion, game, or hands-on)
  3. Snack Break (10 min): Assign a rotating snack family each week
  4. Activity 2 (15–20 min): Craft, game, or continuation of Activity 1
  5. Closing (5–10 min): Friendship circle, Friendship Squeeze, announcements

Total: 60–75 minutes. That's plenty for this age. Don't try for 90 minutes — you'll lose them.

Essential Supplies to Have Ready

  • Daisy vest or tunic (each girl gets one; parents purchase from the GS Shop)
  • Petal and badge iron-ons (order from gsstore.org)
  • A small American flag and troop flag/number
  • Basic craft supplies: markers, glue sticks, construction paper, scissors
  • A first aid kit
  • Roster and emergency contact info (printed backup)
  • Snack sign-up sheet

Money & Dues

Most troops collect small dues ($1–3/meeting or ~$20/semester) to cover craft supplies and badges. Cookie season funds bigger activities. Be transparent with parents about where money goes. Your council may also provide start-up funds for new troops.

🎈 Keeping It Fun (and Not Overwhelming)

For the Girls:

  • Variety. Alternate between active/physical, crafty/creative, and learning/discussion activities.
  • Movement. 6-year-olds cannot sit still for 20 minutes. Build in movement every 15 min.
  • Songs & silliness. Learn 2–3 Girl Scout songs. They love repetition.
  • Outdoor time. Even 10 minutes outside transforms the energy.
  • Celebrate everything. Earning a petal is a big deal. Make it feel like one.

For You (the Leaders):

  • Prep takes 30 min, not 3 hours. Use VTK meeting plans. Don't reinvent the wheel.
  • Lower your standards (lovingly). The craft will look messy. That's the point.
  • Delegate to parents. Snacks, setup, cleanup, driving — parents want to help.
  • You don't need to earn every badge. 6–8 petals in a season is great. Don't chase all 10.
  • Bad meetings happen. A meeting where everyone cried and the craft failed is still a meeting. They won't remember it. Move on.
⚠️ First-time leader trap: Over-planning. You spend 4 hours preparing a perfect meeting, one girl has a meltdown, another spills glue everywhere, and you finish only half your plan. This is normal. Over-prepare the first 2 meetings, then you'll find your groove.

📚 Key GSUSA Resources

  • Volunteer Toolkit (VTK) — Your best friend. Has complete meeting plans for every petal and badge. Log in with your GSUSA credentials.
  • Badge Explorer — Browse all available Daisy badges and requirements.
  • Your council website — Local events, training, camping opportunities, and council-specific resources.
  • Girl Scout Shop — Uniforms, badges, and supplies.
  • "Welcome to the Daisy Flower Garden" Journey book — The main Daisy journey; available at the GS Shop.
💡 Pro tip: Your council likely offers a "New Leader Training" event. Go. Even if it feels basic, you'll meet other leaders and get council-specific info you can't find online. Many councils also have a Facebook group for local leaders — gold mine for advice.
